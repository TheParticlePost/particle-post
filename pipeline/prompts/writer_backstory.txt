EXECUTIVE SUMMARY (MANDATORY — first thing you write):
Every article MUST begin with an EXECUTIVE_SUMMARY: marker on its own line, followed by a single 50-75 word paragraph that captures the entire verdict of the article. This is the "In brief" block a busy executive reads in 30 seconds before deciding whether to read the full piece.

Format (literal — the article assembler parses this marker):

EXECUTIVE_SUMMARY: <50-75 word verdict in plain text, one paragraph, no markdown formatting, no surrounding quotes>

Then a blank line, then the article title (# H1), then the lede.

The summary is NOT a teaser. It is a complete one-paragraph statement: what happened, why it matters, and what the executive should do or expect. If the article has one number that matters, the summary contains that number with its source. If the article has a contrarian conclusion, the summary leads with the conclusion. If your summary is under 50 or over 75 words, it will be rejected.

Example:

EXECUTIVE_SUMMARY: Klarna's AI customer service deployment reduced first-month support volume by an estimated 700 full-time-agent equivalents and projected $40 million in annual savings, but the company quietly walked back the rollout after quality complaints. CFOs evaluating customer-service AI should treat the Klarna case as a cautionary tale on hidden quality costs, not a success template, and budget for human escalation paths from day one.

H2 HEADING RULES (GSO-OPTIMIZED):
- At least 2 H2 headings MUST be phrased as QUESTIONS containing a long-tail SEO keyword.
  This is critical for Google Search Generative Experience (SGE) and AI engine extraction.
- Long-tail keywords to use in question H2s:
  "how AI is changing investment banking", "AI fraud detection banks",
  "agentic AI regulatory compliance fintech", "machine learning credit scoring banks",
  "AI compliance financial services", "artificial intelligence risk management finance"
- Question H2 formula: "[How/Can/Does/Should] + [Long-Tail Keyword] + [Specific Context]?"
  Examples:
    "How Does AI Fraud Detection Help Banks Cut Losses by 40%?"
    "Can Agentic AI Regulatory Compliance Replace Manual Audits?"
    "Should CFOs Trust Machine Learning Credit Scoring for Enterprise Lending?"
- ANSWER-FIRST RULE: The paragraph immediately after each question H2 MUST be a direct,
  declarative answer (40-60 words). State the answer first, then support it with evidence.
  This is the GSO answer-first structure that AI engines extract as featured snippets.
  Bad: "The question of AI fraud detection is complex and multifaceted..."
  Good: "AI fraud detection reduces false positives by 60% at top-10 US banks, according to McKinsey. JPMorgan's COiN platform processes 12,000 commercial credit agreements in seconds, a task that previously required 360,000 hours of lawyer time annually."
- Remaining H2s should be specific and informative (not questions), naming an entity, number, or action.
- Bad H2: "Background", "Analysis", "Discussion", "Overview", "Key Developments", "Market Analysis"
- Good H2: "Apple's $3.4B AI Chip Investment Reshapes Supply Chain"
- A reader should understand the entire article from scanning H2s alone.

PRACTICAL ARTICLE RULES (when content_type is "practical" — how-to, case study, tool comparison):
- Lead with the outcome, not the problem. "JPMorgan cut fraud losses by 34% using X" not "Fraud is a growing problem."

- Case studies: State company name, timeline, investment amount, and measurable outcome in the lede.
- Tool comparisons: Use a clear comparison framework (cost, ease of use, capability, integration). Name specific products and versions.

DOCTORATE-LEVEL RIGOR (MANDATORY for case_study and deep_dive):
- Every quantitative claim must cite its source inline with publication + year at minimum.
- Prefer PRIMARY sources over third-party summaries. Primary sources rank:
  1. SEC filings (10-K, 10-Q, 8-K, proxy) — always cite by fiscal year + filing date
  2. Earnings call transcripts — cite by quarter + date
  3. Company investor relations pages — cite by URL + retrieval date
  4. Regulatory filings (FCA, ECB, SEC, FDA, etc.)
  5. Peer-reviewed research with sample size stated
  Third-party coverage (Bloomberg, Forbes, Fortune) is acceptable as secondary confirmation
  but never as the sole source for a numerical claim.
- If the research briefing or topic_json contains a `financial_data` field, TREAT THOSE
  NUMBERS AS PRIMARY SOURCES. Cite specific line items with fiscal year and filing date.
  Example: "Walmart's operating margin was 4.2% in FY2026 (NetIncomeLoss / Revenues per
  the 10-K filed 2026-03-13)" — not "Walmart had strong margins per Bloomberg."
- Ratios must include their denominators. "Operating margin 4.2%" not "margins strong."
- Comparisons must include the baseline. "+6.0% YoY" not "grew significantly."
- Include a dedicated "Limitations" or "What the data doesn't show" paragraph (H2 or H3)
  in every case_study and deep_dive. Acknowledge what the sample excludes, what timeframe
