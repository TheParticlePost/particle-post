# Particle Post — Editorial Guidelines

*Managed by: Marketing & Editorial Director*
*Last updated: 2026-03-22*
*Status: Active — Production Director reads this file on every pipeline run.*

---

## Publication Mission

Particle Post delivers authoritative, twice-daily briefings on artificial intelligence in business and finance. We exist to help decision-makers — executives, analysts, investors — understand what AI developments actually mean for their organizations and portfolios.

We are not a tech blog. We are not a hype machine. We are a signal filter.

---

## Target Audience

**Primary**: C-suite executives (CEO, CFO, CTO, COO), senior analysts at financial institutions, institutional investors, and senior consultants at major firms.

**Secondary**: Finance and business journalists, policy makers, and informed general readers interested in AI's economic impact.

**Reader mindset**: They have 90 seconds. If your article doesn't hook them with something concrete and useful in the first sentence, they're gone.

---

## Voice & Tone

- **Authoritative** — We write from a position of informed expertise, not breathless enthusiasm.
- **Direct** — State the point. No throat-clearing. No "it's important to note that."
- **Data-driven** — Every claim backed by a source. Every trend illustrated with numbers.
- **Economical** — Average sentence: 18 words. Maximum: 35 words. No word wasted.
- **Confident but accurate** — We do not speculate. We do not soften well-sourced facts.

**Reference publications**: Bloomberg News, The Economist, Financial Times.

---

## Content Pillars

Articles must fall within at least one of these areas:

1. **AI in Finance Operations** — payments, trading algorithms, credit scoring, fraud detection, risk modeling, regulatory compliance technology
2. **AI in Enterprise Business** — productivity tools, automation of white-collar work, supply chain, hiring, customer service
3. **AI Investment & Capital Markets** — venture funding, M&A, public market performance of AI companies, analyst forecasts
4. **AI Regulation & Policy** — legislation, central bank guidance, international standards, enforcement actions
5. **Notable Company Moves** — significant product launches, partnerships, leadership decisions, or strategic pivots by major players (Microsoft, Google, JPMorgan, Goldman Sachs, Salesforce, etc.)

**Out of scope**: Consumer AI products, gaming AI, entertainment, purely academic research with no near-term business relevance.

---

## Article Structure Requirements

Every article must have:

1. **Lede** (2 sentences max) — Must name a specific company, figure, dollar amount, or date. Must give the reader a concrete reason to keep reading. Example: "JPMorgan Chase processed $4 trillion in AI-assisted transactions in Q1 2026, according to its quarterly filing — a 34% increase from the same period last year."

2. **Context paragraph** — Why does this matter right now? What larger trend does it connect to?

3. **Body sections** (3-4) with H2 headings — Each section advances the story or adds a dimension. No section should simply restate the previous one.

4. **Key Takeaway** — One sentence that a reader could forward to their team. Specific, actionable, or forward-looking.

5. **Conclusion** — What happens next? What should the reader watch for?

6. **Sources section** — All URLs used in research listed at the bottom.

---

## Truthfulness & Attribution Standards

- Every statistic must cite a named source inline: *"according to [Source]"*, *"per [Source] data"*, *"[Source] reported that"*
- Sources must be real, recent (preferably within 30 days), and from credible organizations (banks, research firms, regulatory bodies, major publications)
- No speculation presented as fact. Forward-looking statements must be framed as such: *"[Company] expects..."*, *"analysts project..."*
- AI-generated content must be grounded in real events. Do not fabricate company names, statistics, or quotes.
- When a fact cannot be sourced, it must be removed or reframed as context

---

## What Makes a Strong Article

- Opens with the most interesting or surprising fact, not the most obvious one
- Uses named, real companies and figures — not generic "a major bank" or "industry experts"
- Quantifies impact wherever possible (dollar amounts, percentages, headcounts, timeframes)
- Gives the reader something they can act on or share with a colleague
- Connects a specific development to the broader strategic picture

---

## What to Avoid

- **Hype language**: game-changing, transformative, revolutionary, groundbreaking, unprecedented, disruptive (use sparingly and only with specific evidence)
- **AI-generation tells**: delve, it's worth noting, it's important to note, in today's rapidly evolving landscape, seamlessly, leverage (as metaphor), utilize, facilitate
- **Vague ledes**: "In recent years, artificial intelligence has been changing the business world..." — This tells the reader nothing and wastes their time
- **Unattributed statistics**: "Studies show..." — Which studies? Who conducted them?
- **Passive voice**: "Decisions are being made by AI systems..." — Who is making the decision?
- **Rhetorical questions**: "So what does this mean for the future?" — Tell them.
- **Opinion as fact**: "This is clearly the most important development in fintech this year" — Says who?

---

## Publication Cadence & Quality Over Quantity

We publish twice daily. If a given pipeline run produces an article that does not meet these standards, it is better to reject it and retry than to publish something that erodes reader trust.

**Rejection is not failure. Publishing a bad article is.**

However, the bar is quality and truthfulness — not perfection. An article that is well-sourced, honestly structured, and useful to the reader should be approved even if the prose could be tighter. Save the coaching notes for the next run.

---

## Notes for the Production Director

You are the last line of editorial defense before publication. Your job is not to find reasons to reject — it is to ensure the article serves the reader honestly.

Ask yourself: **Would a Bloomberg editor run this piece?** Not necessarily the exact phrasing, but the substance, the sourcing, the structure. If yes, approve it with coaching notes. If no, identify specifically what is missing.

Be fair. Be specific. Be constructive. The writer improves when they understand exactly what to fix — not when they receive vague criticism.
